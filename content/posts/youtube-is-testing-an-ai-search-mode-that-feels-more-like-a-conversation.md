<p>Google is determined to impose AI search onto <a data-i13n="cpos:1;pos:1" href="https://www.engadget.com/ai/google-brings-gemini-powered-content-creation-tools-to-docs-sheets-slides-and-drive-144705622.html">as many of its products</a> as possible, and the latest, er, victim is YouTube. A new feature called &quot;Ask YouTube&quot; will let you pose complex questions and receive &quot;comprehensive results that include video and text, then ask follow ups to dive deeper,&quot; Google explained on its <a data-i13n="cpos:2;pos:1" href="https://www.youtube.com/new">YouTube Labs page</a>. The experimental feature is available starting today until June 8 for Premium US subscribers 18 and older.&nbsp;</p>
<p>To use it, first, enable the feature in your account. Then, click on the new &quot;Ask YouTube&quot; button in the search bar and you&#39;ll see prompt suggestions, or you can enter your own, like &quot;plan a 3-day road trip between San Francisco and Santa Barbara.&quot; After getting the results, you can try follow-up questions or choose from suggested prompts to explore in more detail.&nbsp;</p>
<span id="end-legacy-contents"></span><p>As shown in <em>The Verge</em>&#39;s<a data-i13n="cpos:3;pos:1" href="https://www.theverge.com/streaming/919441/google-ask-youtube-ai-chatbot-search"> quick test</a>, the prompt &quot;short history of Apollo 11 moon landing&quot; brought up a summary of the mission, along with videos and time stamps for relevant information. Follow-up questions yielded similar results, but some queries just showed a list of videos like you&#39;d see in a classic YouTube search. As happens with AI, one of the searches (around a Steam Controller) yielded factually inaccurate information, according to <em>The Verge</em>&#39;s Jay Peters.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Tech companies love AI a lot more than the public, and YouTube users are particularly passionate about hating AI-generated slop. YouTube&#39;s AI search function may fare better with subscribers, but only if it helps them find quality content more quickly.&nbsp;</p>This article originally appeared on Engadget at https://www.engadget.com/apps/youtube-is-testing-an-ai-search-mode-that-feels-more-like-a-conversation-075057461.html?src=rss

<p>Lenovo already has a large roster of <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/gaming/pc/lenovo-legion-go-2-review-the-utility-pc-gaming-handheld-120000533.html?guccounter=1&amp;guce_referrer=aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuZ29vZ2xlLmNvbS8&amp;guce_referrer_sig=AQAAAF28k3xfmfRkWfn8bQMnUSxfDPZY9wL8OsZ7JT-dWUzdrdgmAvzix3Bz3u6VkPZFCE0LP3gq1x8bv-Ois4rQxn9jJdajrBk-pRU-tJh5ICn4rDEGc7J7lXg9sC3_g3vcz420K9rZFclmfNd5kKGq6qWMWRPoJzUOUar40FYE7jxk" data-i13n="cpos:1;pos:1">gaming</a> <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/gaming/pc/legion-go-s-powered-by-steamos-review-a-purpose-built-os-makes-all-the-difference-120059561.html" data-i13n="cpos:2;pos:1">handhelds</a>. However, it seems there&#39;s always room for another because at MWC the company showed off an ambitious concept device based around a flexible OLED display. And while the whole setup looks ungainly, after getting my hands on it, I&#39;m very intrigued by its adaptability.&nbsp;</p><p>Now I&#39;ll be the first to admit that an 11.6-inch display feels oversized on a handheld that, in theory, is meant to be somewhat portable. That said, the beauty of the Legion Go Fold&#39;s screen is that it can be bent in half to create a more appropriately-sized 7.7-inch panel. In this configuration, the system feels a lot less cumbersome while also sporting a more traditional aspect ratio. Then, at a moment&#39;s notice, you can extend the display to provide extra room when you need it.&nbsp;</p><figure><img src="https://d29szjachogqwa.cloudfront.net/images/user-uploaded/go-fold-long-mode.jpg" data-crop-orig-src="https://d29szjachogqwa.cloudfront.net/images/user-uploaded/go-fold-long-mode.jpg" style="height:2000px;width:3333px;" alt="How silly does this look when its flexible display is fully extended in portrait mode?" data-uuid="19ad0075-6a79-4fd1-9b8e-e6474c383fcb"/><figcaption>How silly does this look when its flexible display is fully extended in portrait mode?</figcaption><div class="photo-credit">Sam Rutherford for Engadget</div></figure><p>However, the thing I like most about the Legion Go Fold is that its controllers are detachable and that the tablet has several different mounting points. This means if you have the space, you can remove the controllers, rotate the display into landscape mode and then reattach them to get an immersive widescreen experience. Alternatively, you can connect the gamepads together using one of Lenovo&#39;s accessories (which is similar to <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.nintendo.com/us/store/products/joy-con-grip-105703/?srsltid=AfmBOoq_0yfySMn8Zm4hhzSF9RcoesikW25WNE4rwQ4WJVYZaRK33RlI" data-i13n="cpos:3;pos:1">Nintendo&#39;s Joy-Con Grip</a>) and then prop the system up using the Go Fold&#39;s folio cover, which doubles as a kickstand.&nbsp;</p><p>But wait, there&#39;s more: The handheld also features a strip of pogo pins for connecting a wireless keyboard, effectively transforming the whole setup into a miniature gaming laptop. Depending on how you&#39;re counting, that&#39;s at least four different modes you&#39;re getting from the Legion Fold, which is really quite impressive and speaks to the versatility of Lenovo&#39;s design.</p><p>Another feature I really like is the small 1-inch OLED display on the right gamepad. It supports a handful of widgets that can display the time, performance settings and more. It also doubles as a small touchpad, which can be really handy when playing PC titles that were originally developed for mouse and keyboard. And just like on the <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/lenovo-legion-go-review-the-xl-alternative-to-the-steam-deck-141522230.html" data-i13n="cpos:4;pos:1">Legion Go</a> and<a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/gaming/pc/lenovo-legion-go-2-review-the-utility-pc-gaming-handheld-120000533.html" data-i13n="cpos:5;pos:1"> Legion Go 2</a>, the Fold&#39;s right gamepad has a small scroll wheel and a hidden sensor, so it can also turn into a vertical mouse for playing FPS titles.&nbsp;</p><p>At this point, it&#39;s important to mention that Lenovo doesn&#39;t have concrete plans to put this thing into production, so its specs don&#39;t really matter. That said, the Legion Go Fold I tried featured an Intel Core Ultra 7 258V chip with 32GB of RAM and a 48WHr battery, which is more than respectable. Though if this thing does become an actual retail device, I would like to see a power cell with a bit more capacity.&nbsp;</p><figure><img src="https://d29szjachogqwa.cloudfront.net/images/user-uploaded/go-fold-laptop-mode.jpg" data-crop-orig-src="https://d29szjachogqwa.cloudfront.net/images/user-uploaded/go-fold-laptop-mode.jpg" style="height:2000px;width:3333px;" alt="I really love how the Legion Go Fold can turn into a miniature laptop just by moving some of its accessories around. " data-uuid="1bee91c4-d1f7-4f2e-a6e4-f88b98bf0ab9"/><figcaption>I really love how the Legion Go Fold can turn into a miniature laptop just by moving some of its accessories around. </figcaption><div class="photo-credit">Sam Rutherford for Engadget</div></figure><p>But as a concept gadget, I think the Legion Go Fold is a great showcase of how new technology (in this case, a flexible display) can bring new capabilities and greater flexibility to existing categories. And who knows, if a lot of people like the idea, it might encourage Lenovo to refine it and put it on sale for real. I would just be a bit worried about pricing, because all these components won&#39;t come cheap.&nbsp;<br></p><p></p>This article originally appeared on Engadget at https://www.engadget.com/gaming/pc/the-lenovo-legion-go-fold-gaming-handheld-concept-looks-awkward-but-its-versatility-is-endless-230000816.html?src=rss

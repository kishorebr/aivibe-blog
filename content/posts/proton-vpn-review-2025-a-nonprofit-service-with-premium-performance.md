<p>Proton VPN stands out for two main reasons: it's one of the only virtual private networks (VPNs) to include a free plan with no data limits, and it's one of the few services majority-owned by a nonprofit. It's the best VPN in both of those categories, and it makes a strong case for being the best overall.</p> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Even if you don't care about the work of the Proton Foundation, Proton VPN is a service worth using. It's easy to install and manage, runs like the wind and meets high standards for security and privacy. It has more IP locations in Africa than any of its competitors. It's even looking toward the future by working toward full IPv6 support.</p> <span id="end-legacy-contents"></span> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">In short, Proton VPN gets our enthusiastic recommendation, especially for torrenting (which it supports on almost every server). It's not perfect — the apps for Apple systems lag behind their Windows and Android counterparts, and the free servers can be noticeably sluggish — but the cons pale in comparison to the pros. We'll get into it all below.</p>

<h2 class="" style="" id="jump-link-installing-configuring-and-using-proton-vpn">Installing, configuring and using Proton VPN</h2> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">This section explains how it feels to run Proton VPN on the various platforms it supports. As a rule, it's not difficult. Proton VPN has more features than ExpressVPN, and a couple of them might trip up new users, but you'd have to really scrounge to find an actual inconvenience to complain about.</p> 
<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-windows">Windows</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Proton VPN installs easily on Windows — you'll need to grant it permission to make changes, but that's it. Once you've signed in on the app, you'll reach one of the best interfaces we've tried on a VPN. The server network is immediately visible as both a list and map, and the location search bar, connect button and major features are all laid out around the same window.</p>

<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">It's honestly amazing how much you can reach from the launch window without anything feeling cluttered. They even squeezed in keyboard shortcuts for the search field. We also love that settings open in the same window, since dealing with both the main VPN app and a separate preferences panel can get annoying. Our only real gripe is that there should be an easier way to adjust the size of the map.</p> 
<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-mac">Mac</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">The desktop app for Mac isn't quite as deftly laid out as the Windows app. You can reach most of the important features from the main window, including Profiles, NetShield, Secure Core servers and the kill switch. However, there's no longer a way to filter out a list of the P2P or Tor servers, except by digging through the Profile controls. On the plus side, you can adjust the size of the map, so it's now a viable alternative to the server list.</p>

<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-android">Android</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">The Android app takes the same design cues as the Windows app, and works as well. Four tabs along the bottom switch between the home screen, the country list, Profiles and all other settings. You can search the list of countries by tapping the magnifying glass at the top-right. In another nice touch, tapping the dots by any virtual location will tell you where the server is physically located.</p>

<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-ios">iOS</h3>

But these are minor quibbles — this is still a VPN that's very easy to activate and forget about.</p> 
<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-browser-extensions">Browser extensions</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Proton VPN has browser extensions for Chrome and Firefox. These serve as de facto split tunneling, as connecting through the extension protects only browser traffic; everything else goes unprotected. You can split the tunnel further by setting the VPN to not work on certain URLs (unlike the other apps, you don't need to know the IPs of those sites).</p> 
<h2 class="" style="" id="jump-link-proton-vpn-speed-test-impacts-of-vpn-accelerator">Proton VPN speed test: Impacts of VPN Accelerator</h2> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">We used Ookla's Speedtest app to determine how much Proton VPN drags on a user's latency, measured in milliseconds (ms), and download and upload speeds, measured in megabits per second (Mbps). Together, these three stats show whether a VPN will noticeably slow down your internet, especially during demanding tasks.</p> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">We had high hopes for Proton here because of its VPN Accelerator technology, which runs VPN communications across several parallel tracks to process everything faster. It didn't disappoint — for the most part. Although download speeds didn't reach the heights <a data-i13n="cpos:17;pos:1" href="https://www.engadget.com/vpn-review-expressvpn-2023-gaming-streaming-160052492.html">we saw from ExpressVPN</a>, Proton VPN's browsing performance still looks excellent nearly across the board.</p>

<h2 class="" style="" id="jump-link-proton-vpn-security-test-watertight-protocols">Proton VPN security test: Watertight protocols</h2> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">When we talk about VPN security, we're really talking about reliability. Can this VPN establish an encrypted tunnel and transmit all your information through it, every time, without leaks or failures? With Proton VPN, we're happy to say the answer is yes; we probed its security and found no cracks to speak of. Read the section below for specifics.</p> 
<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-proton-vpn-protocols-wireguard-openvpn-ikev2-and-stealth">Proton VPN protocols: WireGuard, OpenVPN, IKEv2 and Stealth</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Proton VPN uses four VPN protocols to communicate between your devices, its servers and the internet. Three of them (WireGuard, OpenVPN and IKEv2) are common choices with no serious flaws as long as they're implemented thoughtfully. The fourth, Stealth, is an obfuscation protocol you should only use if the other three are blocked.</p>

<ul class="public-DraftStyleDefault-ul" style="margin:16px 0px;padding:0px;color:rgb(32, 43, 51);"> 
 <li class="public-DraftStyleDefault-unorderedListItem public-DraftStyleDefault-reset public-DraftStyleDefault-depth0 public-DraftStyleDefault-listLTR" style="direction:ltr;list-style-type:disc;position:relative;margin-left:1.5em;counter-reset:ol0 0;"><p><strong>OpenVPN </strong>is the most secure option. Without getting too technical, OpenVPN encrypts its backend functions as well as the data itself, which leaves it with no obvious vulnerabilities. It can also communicate using the same ports as common HTTPS traffic, so it's hard to block.</p></li> 
 <li class="public-DraftStyleDefault-unorderedListItem public-DraftStyleDefault-depth0 public-DraftStyleDefault-listLTR" style="direction:ltr;list-style-type:disc;position:relative;margin-left:1.5em;"><p><strong>WireGuard </strong>is more efficient than OpenVPN, both in its source code and the cryptography it uses. It normally requires an exchange of fixed IP addresses, but the Proton VPN implementation overwrites those IPs with randomized addresses, cutting the security risk.</p></li> 
 <li class="public-DraftStyleDefault-unorderedListItem public-DraftStyleDefault-depth0 public-DraftStyleDefault-listLTR" style="direction:ltr;list-style-type:disc;position:relative;margin-left:1.5em;"><p><strong>IKEv2 </strong>is a safe choice that's occasionally faster than either OpenVPN or WireGuard. You probably won't use it unless it happens to come up while you've set the protocol to Smart. As such, it's on the way to being phased out of Proton VPN.</p></li> 
 <li class="public-DraftStyleDefault-unorderedListItem public-DraftStyleDefault-depth0 public-DraftStyleDefault-listLTR" style="direction:ltr;list-style-type:disc;position:relative;margin-left:1.5em;"><p><strong>Stealth </strong>is unique to Proton VPN, though its function is not. It's got the same architecture as WireGuard, but adds another TLS tunnel to evade network blocks that catch VPN traffic. That extra encryption slows it down, so we don't recommend using it unless the other three protocols don't work.</p></li> 
</ul> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">The first option on the app, called "Smart," is not a protocol — it means your VPN client selects the protocol that will give you the best speeds on your current server. Since all four protocols are safe, there's no downside to using the Smart setting most of the time.</p> 
<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-leak-test">Leak test</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">We used <a data-i13n="cpos:19;pos:1" href="https://ipleak.net/">AirVPN's IP leak tool</a> to check all four of Proton VPN's protocols for DNS and WebRTC leaks. Proton VPN uses its own DNS servers to reduce the risk of sending unencrypted requests, but since leaks are still possible, we tested using a simple method: checking our visible IP address before and after connecting to the VPN.</p> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Despite testing several locations over three days, we never saw our real IP address show up on the tool. This held true even outside the Secure Core server list, where data centers might have been managed by third parties. It's not perfect proof, but it's a very good sign that Proton VPN enforces a consistent security regime on all its servers.</p>

<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-proton-vpn-and-ipv6">Proton VPN and IPv6</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Proton is working on making its entire VPN compatible with IPv6 addresses. If a VPN that's only configured for IPv4 has to resolve an IPv6 address, it can cause a leak — that's why most VPNs, even the best ones, block IPv6 altogether. That said, the whole internet will run on IPv6 one day, so it's nice to see Proton VPN leading the pack.</p> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">So far, IPv6 is automatically enabled on Proton VPN's Linux apps and browser extensions, and can be optionally activated on its Android app. The Proton VPN apps for all other platforms still block IPv6 traffic, but this should hopefully change soon.</p> 
<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-encryption-test">Encryption test</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Even if a VPN's protocol choices are solid, it's possible for individual implementations of those protocols to fail. We used WireShark, a packet inspector app, to test whether Proton VPN's encryption worked no matter what settings were in place. We're happy to say we never saw plaintext once.</p>

It's also been confirmed several times by a third-party audit, most recently in <a data-i13n="cpos:26;pos:1" href="https://drive.proton.me/urls/ED8G4GC5MG#pM52Y8RMXIKn">July 2024</a>.</p>

<h2 class="" style="" id="jump-link-features-of-proton-vpn">Features of Proton VPN</h2> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Proton VPN has a number of extra features that go beyond standard VPN functionality. We've already mentioned the Stealth protocol and IPv6 support. In this section, we'll cover an additional five features that might be of interest.</p> 
<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-netshield-ad-blocker">NetShield ad blocker</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Proton VPN's built-in ad blocker is known as NetShield. It's available on the main UI page, and has two settings: one that blocks only malware sites, and one that blocks sites connected with malware, ads and trackers. The stronger feature is on by default.</p>

<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">NetShield works by checking any DNS requests against a database of web servers known to host malware, inject ads or attach cross-site trackers to your browsing session. The DNS-blocking approach means it's not capable of blocking ads served from the same domain that hosts them — so no blocking YouTube video ads. On the positive side, it means NetShield works across your entire device, not just on your browser.</p> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">NetShield also displays a running total of how many of each form of interference it's blocked. It catches most banner ads, but since you can't customize the blocklist in any way, it's best when combined with another browser-level ad blocker.</p> 
<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-secure-core-servers">Secure Core servers</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Here's an interesting one. Secure Core is a form of a common feature known as double VPN or multi-hop VPN, in which a connection runs through two VPN servers before being decrypted. If one server fails or gets compromised, the other server keeps your connection private.</p>

<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Proton VPN takes this a step further. When you activate Secure Core, your connection will still end at your chosen server location — but before that, it will travel through a designated server in Iceland, Sweden or Switzerland. A few touches make Secure Core servers more reliable than the average VPN node:</p> 
<ul class="public-DraftStyleDefault-ul" style="margin:16px 0px;padding:0px;color:rgb(32, 43, 51);"> 
 <li class="public-DraftStyleDefault-unorderedListItem public-DraftStyleDefault-reset public-DraftStyleDefault-depth0 public-DraftStyleDefault-listLTR" style="direction:ltr;list-style-type:disc;position:relative;margin-left:1.5em;counter-reset:ol0 0;"><p>All three countries are safe jurisdictions, with consumer-friendly privacy laws and courts sympathetic to privacy claims.</p></li> 
 <li class="public-DraftStyleDefault-unorderedListItem public-DraftStyleDefault-depth0 public-DraftStyleDefault-listLTR" style="direction:ltr;list-style-type:disc;position:relative;margin-left:1.5em;"><p>Secure Core data centers are locked down physically; for example, the Iceland location is a refitted military base, and the Sweden location is literally underground.</p></li> 
 <li class="public-DraftStyleDefault-unorderedListItem public-DraftStyleDefault-depth0 public-DraftStyleDefault-listLTR" style="direction:ltr;list-style-type:disc;position:relative;margin-left:1.5em;"><p>Proton owns and operates all Secure Core locations itself, with no rentals or third-party managers.</p></li> 
</ul> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Almost no other VPNs pay as much attention to physical security as Proton VPN does with this feature. The second hop makes Secure Core connections slower on average, but it's worth it if you have something especially sensitive to do online.</p> 
<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-kill-switch">Kill switch</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Proton VPN includes a kill switch, a standard feature. When active, a kill switch cuts off your internet whenever your connection to the VPN drops. This means you're never in danger of leaking your real identity or location, even for a second. It also protects you against the TunnelVision exploit, which requires the hacker to make a fake VPN server.</p> 
<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-split-tunneling">Split tunneling</h3>

<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-torrenting-servers-and-port-forwarding">Torrenting servers and port forwarding</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Proton VPN is one of the best VPNs for torrenting. It restricts torrenting to P2P servers, but nearly every server on the list is a P2P server — only Secure Core servers and some free servers don't permit torrenting. <h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-tor-over-vpn">Tor over VPN</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">A handful of Proton VPN servers route you directly to the Tor network after encrypting your connection. While connected to one of these Tor over VPN servers, which are marked with TOR in their names and an onion symbol, you'll be able to open .onion links on a normal browser.</p> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">This is more than just convenient — Tor over VPN is the safest way to access the dark web. With the VPN as an intermediary, you're never connecting to Tor with your own IP address, so malicious node operators can't see your real identity. For maximum privacy, use Tor Browser to create your Proton VPN account, so you're never exposed at any point in the process.</p> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Proton VPN has <a data-i13n="cpos:28;pos:1" href="https://www.engadget.com/tor-dark-web-privacy-secure-browser-anonymous-130048839.html">Tor servers</a> in six countries (the U.S., France, Switzerland, Sweden, Germany and Hong Kong). Each just has one Tor server, except the United States, which gets two. Tor over VPN is supported on all platforms, but it won't work on Mac or iOS unless you have the kill switch enabled.</p> 
<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-profiles">Profiles</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">A "profile" on Proton VPN is a group of pre-established settings you can use to quickly configure the VPN for a particular task. Two profiles are available from the start: Fastest, which connects to the fastest server, and Random, which always connects to a different server. You can create more profiles by toggling four settings:</p> 
<ul class="public-DraftStyleDefault-ul" style="margin:16px 0px;padding:0px;color:rgb(32, 43, 51);"> 
 <li class="public-DraftStyleDefault-unorderedListItem public-DraftStyleDefault-reset public-DraftStyleDefault-depth0 public-DraftStyleDefault-listLTR" style="direction:ltr;list-style-type:disc;position:relative;margin-left:1.5em;counter-reset:ol0 0;"><p><strong>Feature: </strong>The type of server used. Choose from Standard, Secure Core, P2P or Tor over VPN.</p></li> 
 <li class="public-DraftStyleDefault-unorderedListItem public-DraftStyleDefault-depth0 public-DraftStyleDefault-listLTR" style="direction:ltr;list-style-type:disc;position:relative;margin-left:1.5em;"><p><strong>Country: </strong>The country to which the profile connects.</p></li> 
 <li class="public-DraftStyleDefault-unorderedListItem public-DraftStyleDefault-depth0 public-DraftStyleDefault-listLTR" style="direction:ltr;list-style-type:disc;position:relative;margin-left:1.5em;"><p><strong>Server: </strong>A server within that country. You can also select "fastest" or "random."</p></li> 
 <li class="public-DraftStyleDefault-unorderedListItem public-DraftStyleDefault-depth0 public-DraftStyleDefault-listLTR" style="direction:ltr;list-style-type:disc;position:relative;margin-left:1.5em;"><p><strong>Protocol: </strong>Which VPN protocol the profile will use. "Smart" can be selected.</p></li> 
</ul> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">As an example, say you want to watch a TV series that's only available on Netflix in Canada. You could create a profile called "Netflix Canada" that connects to the fastest Canadian server with just one click. We'd call profiles situationally useful, but they can save a lot of time if you regularly perform the same action on your VPN.</p>

The founders of its parent company, Proton AG, met while working at CERN in Switzerland, and the company remains under Swiss jurisdiction.</p> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Their first product, Proton Mail, <a data-i13n="elm:affiliate_link;sellerN:ProtonVPN;elmt:;cpos:30;pos:1" href="https://shopping.yahoo.com/rdlw?merchantId=06a20697-4135-4e83-a421-f326eee937ed&amp;siteId=us-engadget&amp;pageId=1p-autolink&amp;contentUuid=e57a73d9-fba2-42bc-9eff-80681f65291d&amp;featureId=text-link&amp;merchantName=ProtonVPN&amp;linkText=went+live+in+2014&amp;custData=eyJzb3VyY2VOYW1lIjoiV2ViLURlc2t0b3AtVmVyaXpvbiIsImxhbmRpbmdVcmwiOiJodHRwczovL3Byb3Rvbi5tZS9ibG9nL3Byb3Rvbm1haWwtcmFpc2VzLWEtcmVjb3JkLTUwMDAwMC10aHJvdWdoLWNyb3dkZnVuZGluZy10by1kaXNydXB0LXRoZS1zdGF0ZS1vZi1lbWFpbC1jb21tdW5pY2F0aW9ucyIsImNvbnRlbnRVdWlkIjoiZTU3YTczZDktZmJhMi00MmJjLTllZmYtODA2ODFmNjUyOTFkIiwib3JpZ2luYWxVcmwiOiJodHRwczovL3Byb3Rvbi5tZS9ibG9nL3Byb3Rvbm1haWwtcmFpc2VzLWEtcmVjb3JkLTUwMDAwMC10aHJvdWdoLWNyb3dkZnVuZGluZy10by1kaXNydXB0LXRoZS1zdGF0ZS1vZi1lbWFpbC1jb21tdW5pY2F0aW9ucyJ9&amp;signature=AQAAAQ7KgDfpT6-aA8s4V_ufKNSFes-f2RZ9J9juKl_rPkDB&amp;gcReferrer=https%3A%2F%2Fproton.me%2Fblog%2Fprotonmail-raises-a-record-500000-through-crowdfunding-to-disrupt-the-state-of-email-communications" class="rapid-with-clickid" data-original-link="https://proton.me/blog/protonmail-raises-a-record-500000-through-crowdfunding-to-disrupt-the-state-of-email-communications">went live in 2014</a> after a successful crowdfunding campaign, and claims to have 100 million users today. Proton VPN was Proton AG's second project. Like Proton Mail, it consists of a free plan supplemented by paid upgrades.</p> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Since then, Proton has introduced several more products: Proton Calendar in 2020, Proton Drive cloud storage in 2022 and the Proton Pass password manager in 2023, each designed around using end-to-end encryption to make user data inaccessible.</p> 
<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-the-proton-foundation">The Proton Foundation</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Proton <a data-i13n="elm:affiliate_link;sellerN:ProtonVPN;elmt:;cpos:31;pos:1" href="https://shopping.yahoo.com/rdlw?merchantId=06a20697-4135-4e83-a421-f326eee937ed&amp;siteId=us-engadget&amp;pageId=1p-autolink&amp;contentUuid=e57a73d9-fba2-42bc-9eff-80681f65291d&amp;featureId=text-link&amp;merchantName=ProtonVPN&amp;linkText=announced+in+2024&amp;custData=eyJzb3VyY2VOYW1lIjoiV2ViLURlc2t0b3AtVmVyaXpvbiIsImxhbmRpbmdVcmwiOiJodHRwczovL3Byb3Rvbi5tZS9ibG9nL3Byb3Rvbi1ub24tcHJvZml0LWZvdW5kYXRpb24iLCJjb250ZW50VXVpZCI6ImU1N2E3M2Q5LWZiYTItNDJiYy05ZWZmLTgwNjgxZjY1MjkxZCIsIm9yaWdpbmFsVXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9wcm90b24ubWUvYmxvZy9wcm90b24tbm9uLXByb2ZpdC1mb3VuZGF0aW9uIn0&amp;signature=AQAAAQK9JTDUuF9w5nimbc3DfyoYowdwUYK_BcKkiYU-Mxcs&amp;gcReferrer=https%3A%2F%2Fproton.me%2Fblog%2Fproton-non-profit-foundation" class="rapid-with-clickid" data-original-link="https://proton.me/blog/proton-non-profit-foundation">announced in 2024</a> that the majority of its shares had been acquired by the Proton Foundation, a nonprofit whose only purpose is to control Proton stock. Among other benefits, this prevents it from being purchased by anyone who disagrees with its mission. To sell to an objectionable parent company, the entire board of trustees would have to agree, which feels unlikely based on Proton's track record.</p> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Throughout Proton's history, we only found two incidents serious enough to comment on, and only one of them concerned Proton VPN. We'll cover them both below.</p> 
<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-protonmail-law-enforcement-collaboration-allegations">ProtonMail law enforcement collaboration allegations</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">On the page that hosts its <a data-i13n="elm:affiliate_link;sellerN:ProtonVPN;elmt:;cpos:32;pos:1" href="https://shopping.yahoo.com/rdlw?merchantId=06a20697-4135-4e83-a421-f326eee937ed&amp;siteId=us-engadget&amp;pageId=1p-autolink&amp;contentUuid=e57a73d9-fba2-42bc-9eff-80681f65291d&amp;featureId=text-link&amp;merchantName=ProtonVPN&amp;linkText=annual+transparency+report&amp;custData=eyJzb3VyY2VOYW1lIjoiV2ViLURlc2t0b3AtVmVyaXpvbiIsImxhbmRpbmdVcmwiOiJodHRwczovL3Byb3Rvbi5tZS9sZWdhbC90cmFuc3BhcmVuY3kiLCJjb250ZW50VXVpZCI6ImU1N2E3M2Q5LWZiYTItNDJiYy05ZWZmLTgwNjgxZjY1MjkxZCIsIm9yaWdpbmFsVXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9wcm90b24ubWUvbGVnYWwvdHJhbnNwYXJlbmN5In0&amp;signature=AQAAAUguEr0eWnSk9B1XXbpX_IOH_vqrrX87vJAe91lCtrqX&amp;gcReferrer=https%3A%2F%2Fproton.me%2Flegal%2Ftransparency" class="rapid-with-clickid" data-original-link="https://proton.me/legal/transparency">annual transparency report</a>, Proton states openly that it "may be legally compelled to disclose certain user information to Swiss authorities" (see the Privacy Policy section of this article for more on precisely what information that describes). In 2021, <a data-i13n="cpos:33;pos:1" href="https://www.theregister.com/2021/09/07/protonmail_hands_user_ip_address_police/">the company admitted</a> it had given Swiss police (acting on a French warrant) a ProtonMail user's IP address and device logs. The police arrested the user, a French environmental activist.</p> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">While that's unnerving for privacy-minded users, there are some important contextual issues to consider. Most importantly, ProtonMail is not governed by the same policy as Proton VPN. At the time the case unfolded, Swiss law <a data-i13n="cpos:34;pos:1" href="https://techcrunch.com/2021/09/06/protonmail-logged-ip-address-of-french-activist-after-order-by-swiss-authorities/">obliged all email companies</a> to comply with court orders from Swiss authorities to hand over data. <a data-i13n="elm:affiliate_link;sellerN:ProtonVPN;elmt:;cpos:35;pos:1" href="https://shopping.yahoo.com/rdlw?merchantId=06a20697-4135-4e83-a421-f326eee937ed&amp;siteId=us-engadget&amp;pageId=1p-autolink&amp;contentUuid=e57a73d9-fba2-42bc-9eff-80681f65291d&amp;featureId=text-link&amp;merchantName=ProtonVPN&amp;linkText=VPNs+aren%27t+subject+to+those+retention+requirements&amp;custData=eyJzb3VyY2VOYW1lIjoiV2ViLURlc2t0b3AtVmVyaXpvbiIsImxhbmRpbmdVcmwiOiJodHRwczovL3Byb3RvbnZwbi5jb20vYmxvZy90cmFuc3BhcmVuY3ktcmVwb3J0LyIsImNvbnRlbnRVdWlkIjoiZTU3YTczZDktZmJhMi00MmJjLTllZmYtODA2ODFmNjUyOTFkIiwib3JpZ2luYWxVcmwiOiJodHRwczovL3Byb3RvbnZwbi5jb20vYmxvZy90cmFuc3BhcmVuY3ktcmVwb3J0LyJ9&amp;signature=AQAAAbfT9m3zG1c8D7UdDj8NVZh0vk1MlSrUw2mjB-ikkIb_&amp;gcReferrer=https%3A%2F%2Fprotonvpn.com%2Fblog%2Ftransparency-report%2F" class="rapid-with-clickid" data-original-link="https://protonvpn.com/blog/transparency-report/">VPNs aren't subject to those retention requirements</a>.</p> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">Today, Swiss email companies have been reclassified so they're also exempt from data retention requirements, thanks in part to <a data-i13n="elm:affiliate_link;sellerN:ProtonVPN;elmt:;cpos:36;pos:1" href="https://shopping.yahoo.com/rdlw?merchantId=06a20697-4135-4e83-a421-f326eee937ed&amp;siteId=us-engadget&amp;pageId=1p-autolink&amp;contentUuid=e57a73d9-fba2-42bc-9eff-80681f65291d&amp;featureId=text-link&amp;merchantName=ProtonVPN&amp;linkText=a+policy+change+Proton+fought+for&amp;custData=eyJzb3VyY2VOYW1lIjoiV2ViLURlc2t0b3AtVmVyaXpvbiIsImxhbmRpbmdVcmwiOiJodHRwczovL3Byb3Rvbi5tZS9ibG9nL2NvdXJ0LXN0cmVuZ3RoZW5zLWVtYWlsLXByaXZhY3kiLCJjb250ZW50VXVpZCI6ImU1N2E3M2Q5LWZiYTItNDJiYy05ZWZmLTgwNjgxZjY1MjkxZCIsIm9yaWdpbmFsVXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9wcm90b24ubWUvYmxvZy9jb3VydC1zdHJlbmd0aGVucy1lbWFpbC1wcml2YWN5In0&amp;signature=AQAAAX8sbJDi0PL7b553Wxlz4-SfyZnXUnRevYo4wI5SefSP&amp;gcReferrer=https%3A%2F%2Fproton.me%2Fblog%2Fcourt-strengthens-email-privacy" class="rapid-with-clickid" data-original-link="https://proton.me/blog/court-strengthens-email-privacy">a policy change Proton fought for</a>. A representative from Proton confirmed that "under Swiss law, we are not obligated to save any user connection logs." It's also reassuring that, despite complying with the subpoena, <a data-i13n="cpos:37;pos:1" href="https://www.theverge.com/2021/9/6/22659861/protonmail-swiss-court-order-french-climate-activist-arrest-identification">Proton wasn't able to turn over the contents of any emails.</a></p> 
<h3 class="" style="" id="jump-link-alleged-wireguard-memory-vulnerability">Alleged WireGuard memory vulnerability</h3> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">In January 2025, researchers at Venak Security alleged that Proton VPN <a data-i13n="cpos:38;pos:1" href="https://venaksecurity.com/2025/01/27/proton-vpn-and-proton-pass-are-suffering-from-memory-protection-user-data-at-risk/">lacks memory protection</a> for keys generated under the WireGuard protocol, which might let hackers scrape the keys and decrypt intercepted communications. Proton responded to Venak in a <a data-i13n="elm:affiliate_link;sellerN:ProtonVPN;elmt:;cpos:39;pos:1" href="https://shopping.yahoo.com/rdlw?merchantId=06a20697-4135-4e83-a421-f326eee937ed&amp;siteId=us-engadget&amp;pageId=1p-autolink&amp;contentUuid=e57a73d9-fba2-42bc-9eff-80681f65291d&amp;featureId=text-link&amp;merchantName=ProtonVPN&amp;linkText=blog+post&amp;custData=eyJzb3VyY2VOYW1lIjoiV2ViLURlc2t0b3AtVmVyaXpvbiIsImxhbmRpbmdVcmwiOiJodHRwczovL3Byb3Rvbi5tZS9zdXBwb3J0L3ZlbmFrLWZhbHNlLWNsYWltcyIsImNvbnRlbnRVdWlkIjoiZTU3YTczZDktZmJhMi00MmJjLTllZmYtODA2ODFmNjUyOTFkIiwib3JpZ2luYWxVcmwiOiJodHRwczovL3Byb3Rvbi5tZS9zdXBwb3J0L3ZlbmFrLWZhbHNlLWNsYWltcyJ9&amp;signature=AQAAATV0a-z6BM-3Ck2Mc0A_TQrF_m_ivzX6tci_HPl_MshV&amp;gcReferrer=https%3A%2F%2Fproton.me%2Fsupport%2Fvenak-false-claims" class="rapid-with-clickid" data-original-link="https://proton.me/support/venak-false-claims">blog post</a>, which a Proton representative confirmed remains their official response to the allegations.</p> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">In short: the Venak article only demonstrates that it's possible to view public keys, not private ones. This isn't much of a bombshell, given that "public" is right there in the name. But asymmetric encryption — the kind used by VPN protocols like WireGuard — requires both keys to decrypt any messages. Even if a hacker were able to get ahold of a private key, they likely wouldn't be able to use it for anything. WireGuard incorporates perfect forward secrecy by default, changing session keys often enough that any given key is obsolete by the time it's stolen.</p> 
<h2 class="" style="" id="jump-link-final-verdict">Final verdict</h2> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">In short, we're prepared to recommend Proton VPN to almost anybody. Whether you're mainly concerned with security, streaming or something else, chances are good that you'll be satisfied. The only serious downsides are that the long-term plans are overpriced and that it's hard to get live tech support if you live outside of Europe.</p> 
<p class="public-DraftStyleDefault-block public-DraftStyleDefault-ltr" style="position:relative;white-space:pre-wrap;direction:ltr;text-align:left;">It's also our unqualified pick for the best free VPN, but with the caveat that it's a bad choice for anyone who needs to choose specific server locations.

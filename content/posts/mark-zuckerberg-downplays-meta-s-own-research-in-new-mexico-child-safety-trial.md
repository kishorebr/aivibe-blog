<p>Jurors in a New Mexico child safety trial heard testimony from Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg today. During pre-recorded testimony, Zuckerberg was repeatedly asked about the company&#39;s understanding of social media addiction and other issues that had been studied by its researchers.&nbsp;</p><p>During the deposition, which was recorded last March, Zuckerberg was asked about numerous findings from researchers at Meta who studied how the company&#39;s apps affect users and teens. The CEO downplayed the significance of many of these documents.</p><p>Early in the testimony, which was viewed by Engadget on Courtroom View Network, Zuckerberg was questioned about a document on the effect of feedback on Facebook users. The document stated that &quot;contributors on Facebook are likely to learn to associate the act of posting with feedback&quot; which will &quot;lead contributors to seek rewards by visiting the site more often.” Zuckerberg said he wasn’t “sure if that&#39;s actually how it works in practice, but I agree that you&#39;re summarizing what they appear to be saying.”</p><p>Later, the CEO was questioned about a document that graphed the proportion of 11 and 12-year-olds who were monthly active users on Instagram. The chart indicated that at the time, around 20 percent of 11-year-olds were monthly users of the service. &quot;I agree that the graph says that, I am not familiar with what methodology we were using to estimate this,&quot; Zuckerberg said. &quot;I assume that if we had direct knowledge that any given person was under the age of 13, that we would have them removed from our services.&quot;</p><p>New Mexico&#39;s attorney general sued the company <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/meta-faces-another-lawsuit-over-child-safety-164732291.html" data-i13n="cpos:1;pos:1">in 2023</a> for alleged lapses in child safety, including facilitating predators&#39; access to minors and building features it knew were addictive. In court, Meta&#39;s lawyers and executives <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/social-media/meta-really-wants-you-to-believe-social-media-addiction-is-not-a-thing-130000927.html" data-i13n="cpos:2;pos:1">have disputed</a> the idea that social media should be considered an &quot;addiction.&quot; In public statements, the company has said that lawsuits have relied on &quot;cherry-picked quotes and snippets of conversations taken out of context&quot; and that it &quot;has consistently put teen safety ahead of growth for over a decade.&quot;</p><p>As with his recent testimony in <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/social-media/mark-zuckerberg-testifies-in-social-media-addiction-trial-that-meta-just-wants-instagram-to-be-useful-234332316.html" data-i13n="cpos:3;pos:1">a separate trial</a> over social media addiction in Los Angeles, Zuckerberg repeatedly rejected the &quot;characterization&quot; of questions that were posed to him. And he said that Meta&#39;s goal was to make its apps &quot;useful&quot; rather than to increase the amount of time people spend with them.&nbsp;</p><p>Zuckerberg was also questioned about a document written by a company researcher that stated &quot;there is increasing scientific evidence, particularly in the US, … that the average net effect of Facebook on people&#39;s well being is slightly negative.&quot; The CEO said that &quot;my understanding is that the general consensus view is not that.&quot;</p><p>It&#39;s not the first time a Meta executive has tried to downplay the significance of internal research. The company used a similar strategy <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/facebook-instagram-mental-health-senate-hearing-210317155.html" data-i13n="cpos:4;pos:1">in 2021</a> after former employee turned whistleblower Frances Haugen disclosed documents showing that Facebook&#39;s researchers had found that Instagram made some teen girls <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/facebook-research-instagram-teen-mental-health-slides-003452675.html" data-i13n="cpos:5;pos:1">feel worse</a> about themselves.</p><p>Zuckerberg&#39;s testimony was played one day after jurors heard recorded testimony from Instagram chief Adam Mosseri. The exec was also asked about Haugen&#39;s disclosures and Meta&#39;s response to them. Some of those disclosures were based on &quot;problematic research,&quot; he said. &quot;Most research is surveys. We run hundreds of surveys every month.&quot;<br></p><p></p>This article originally appeared on Engadget at https://www.engadget.com/social-media/mark-zuckerberg-downplays-metas-own-research-in-new-mexico-child-safety-trial-222924340.html?src=rss

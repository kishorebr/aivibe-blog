<p>John Ternus was unavoidable when Apple debuted the Macbook Neo. He kicked off an intimate media event for the Neo, introducing it as a transformative machine for Apple thanks to its low $599 cost ($499 for education customers) and premium build quality. He was <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://abcnews.com/GMA/News/exclusive-apple-executive-details-affordable-new-macbook-neo/story?id=130782617" data-i13n="cpos:1;pos:1">interviewed on Good Morning America</a>, the sort of prominent media feature CEO Tim Cook typically handles. And when I asked Apple workers about the Neo at its launch event, they almost always brought up Ternus’ vision of the laptop.&nbsp;</p><p>For all intents and purposes, Ternus was Apple’s frontman for the MacBook Neo.</p><p>Ternus is slated for his coronation as Apple&#39;s CEO on September 1, and the Neo is not only a feather in his cap, but a likely indication of the company&#39;s approach to products going forward. It’s a sign that Apple is getting more comfortable taking risks.&nbsp;</p><p>Apple lives and dies on its own premium image. It completely gave up on making cheap iPhones like the SE and 5C, and the $599 iPhone 16e and 17e are more expensive than typical mid-range Android phones (though the $249 Apple Watch SE is admittedly one of the cheaper smartwatches around.). It was risky to shove a mobile processor into a full-fledged computer, which could have made it too weak. And it was a gamble to stick with a meager 8GB of RAM, practically sacrilegious within the Apple pantheon. It&#39;s not breaking new ground for product categories, but the Neo, in being a budget laptop at all, is surprisingly un-Apple.</p><figure><img src="https://d29szjachogqwa.cloudfront.net/images/user-uploaded/macbook_neo_3.jpg" data-crop-orig-src="https://d29szjachogqwa.cloudfront.net/images/user-uploaded/macbook_neo_3.jpg" style="height:1587px;width:2500px;" alt="A citrus MacBook Neo on a table outside." data-uuid="d26838e4-309e-4b4d-aaca-81af21d6f952"/><figcaption>A citrus MacBook Neo on a table outside.</figcaption><div class="photo-credit">Devindra Hardawar for Engadget</div></figure><p>And yet, thanks to Ternus&#39;s hardware leadership and Apple&#39;s command of its software, the MacBook Neo has been a resounding success. It has the best build quality, screen, keyboard, speakers and trackpad that I&#39;ve ever seen in a $600 laptop. As <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/computing/laptops/macbook-neo-review-apple-puts-every-600-windows-pc-to-shame-130000878.html" data-i13n="cpos:2;pos:1">I wrote in my review</a>, &quot;every Windows PC maker, <em>including </em>Microsoft, should be ashamed.&quot;&nbsp;</p><p>While we don’t know the full build cost for the Neo, Apple’s margins for selling it will undoubtedly be far slimmer than the MacBook Air or Pro. But the Neo is more than a profit maker. It’s a device that can serve as a gateway to the Apple ecosystem for kids and students. Even better, it could easily tempt over Windows users.</p><p>We can&#39;t give Ternus all the credit for the Neo, of course, there&#39;s an entire team of product managers and engineers below him doing the actual design work. But it&#39;s hard to deny the flex of building a $600 laptop that doesn&#39;t feel like total garbage. The MacBook Neo surprised me, a jaded technology reporter, on practically every level. And its existence makes me wonder how a Ternus-led Apple could continue to iterate without compromising quality or Apple&#39;s signature attention to detail.</p><p>Ternus is the rare Apple engineer who has played a role in almost all of its existing products — in his 25 year tenure, he’s taken charge of building the Mac, iPad, iPhone and Apple Watch. That gives him a unique perspective of where the company could go next, as well as how Apple could stretch its own capabilities. And based on what I’ve seen of the MacBook Neo, it’ll be interesting to see how Apple reshapes itself for the future. </p>This article originally appeared on Engadget at https://www.engadget.com/computing/laptops/the-macbook-neo-is-a-glimpse-into-john-ternuss-apple-170000842.html?src=rss

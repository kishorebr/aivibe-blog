<p>Josh Wardle had a hell of a tough act to follow. His last game, <em>Wordle</em>, became <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/wordle-new-york-times-millions-new-users-151308110.html" data-i13n="cpos:1;pos:1">incredibly popular</a> thanks to its blend of simplicity, clarity and shareability. Over four years (and a seven-figure <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/wordle-nyt-new-york-times-sale-215510156-215510216.html" data-i13n="cpos:2;pos:1">sale of <em>Wordle </em>to <em>The New York Times</em></a>) later, Wardle has released his follow-up game.&nbsp;</p><p>Like <em>Wordle </em>and its myriad clones, <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.parseword.com/" data-i13n="cpos:3;pos:1"><em>Parseword</em></a> offers up one puzzle per day. It taps into the mechanics of cryptic crossword clues. So it might ask you to replace one of the words with a synonym, reverse it or find a homophone. You may need to remove letters from a word or combine it with another one to find the answer. There’s a tutorial to help you get to grips with things and hints are available.&nbsp;</p><p>Wardle told <a target="_blank" class="link rapid-with-clickid" href="https://shopping.yahoo.com/rdlw?merchantId=2c74dc58-9aed-4251-a9fd-8b3521347943&amp;siteId=us-engadget&amp;pageId=1p-autolink&amp;contentUuid=d342384b-4f79-4a3c-8dcb-2db1a4bbcd91&amp;featureId=text-link&amp;merchantName=The+New+Yorker&amp;linkText=The+New+Yorker&amp;custData=eyJzb3VyY2VOYW1lIjoiV2ViLURlc2t0b3AtVmVyaXpvbiIsImxhbmRpbmdVcmwiOiJodHRwczovL3d3dy5uZXd5b3JrZXIuY29tL2N1bHR1cmUvdGhlLWxlZGUvdGhlLWNyZWF0b3Itb2Ytd29yZGxlLXRyaWVzLXRvLXNvbHZlLXRoZS1jcnlwdGljLWNyb3Nzd29yZCIsImNvbnRlbnRVdWlkIjoiZDM0MjM4NGItNGY3OS00YTNjLThkY2ItMmRiMWE0YmJjZDkxIiwib3JpZ2luYWxVcmwiOiJodHRwczovL3d3dy5uZXd5b3JrZXIuY29tL2N1bHR1cmUvdGhlLWxlZGUvdGhlLWNyZWF0b3Itb2Ytd29yZGxlLXRyaWVzLXRvLXNvbHZlLXRoZS1jcnlwdGljLWNyb3Nzd29yZCJ9&amp;signature=AQAAATpHk5u_21GarfMhyueGJ_9SkDS2i2nNB2EidWKI2JE6&amp;gcReferrer=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.newyorker.com%2Fculture%2Fthe-lede%2Fthe-creator-of-wordle-tries-to-solve-the-cryptic-crossword" data-i13n="elm:affiliate_link;sellerN:The New Yorker;elmt:;slk:The New Yorker;cpos:4;pos:1" data-original-link="https://www.newyorker.com/culture/the-lede/the-creator-of-wordle-tries-to-solve-the-cryptic-crossword"><em>The New Yorker</em></a> he was inspired by <em>The Last of Us</em> showrunner <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/entertainment/streaming/hbo-is-making-a-baldurs-gate-3-tv-show-130000864.html" data-i13n="cpos:5;pos:1">Craig Mazin</a>, who he heard discuss the logic behind cryptics on a podcast. Wardle brought in two friends, Chris Dary and Matt Lee, whom he worked with at Reddit to collaborate on the new game. They received permission to use clues from long-time cryptic constructors Emily Cox and Henry Rathvon.&nbsp;</p><p>After playing the tutorial and today's puzzle, it seems you may need to be wired in a certain way to play <em>Parseword</em>. It’s certainly less approachable than <em>Wordle</em>. At first glance, it’s hard to see this reaching the same kind of mass appeal as Wardle’s previous game. (Fittingly enough, <em>Parseword </em>feels like a game geared toward <em>New Yorker </em>readers.)</p><p>Of course, countless other casual daily games that aim to replicate the success of <em>Wordle</em> have<a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/wordle-clones-have-jumped-the-shark-123231290.html" data-i13n="cpos:6;pos:1"> popped up</a> over the last few years, from <em>Quordle</em>, <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/gaming/bracket-city-could-be-your-latest-word-game-obsession-151405964.html" data-i13n="cpos:7;pos:1"><em>Bracket City</em></a> and <em>Framed</em> to the <em>Times</em>’ own <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/gaming/nyt-connections-bot-uses-ai-to-highlight-each-days-top-mistakes-145242525.html" data-i13n="cpos:8;pos:1"><em>Connections</em></a><em>, Strands </em>and <em>Pips</em>. <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://catfishing.net/" data-i13n="cpos:9;pos:1"><em>Catfishing</em></a>, which asks you to guess Wikipedia article subjects based on their categories, is a new favorite of mine. I still miss the original <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/spotify-will-shut-down-heardle-on-may-5th-151454849.html" data-i13n="cpos:10;pos:1"><em>Heardle</em></a>, though.</p><p><br></p><p></p>This article originally appeared on Engadget at https://www.engadget.com/gaming/wordles-creator-is-back-with-a-new-game-and-its-a-real-chin-scratcher-182639801.html?src=rss

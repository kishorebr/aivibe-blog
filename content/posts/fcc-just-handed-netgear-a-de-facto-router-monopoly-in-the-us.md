<p>The Federal Communications Commission <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DA-26-351A1.pdf" data-i13n="cpos:1;pos:1">has announced</a> that Netgear has been given conditional approval that effectively exempts it from <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/big-tech/the-us-bans-all-new-foreign-made-network-routers-223622966.html" data-i13n="cpos:2;pos:1">a previous ban on foreign-made networking routers</a>. The conditional approval gives the company a de facto — though potentially temporary — monopoly on the selling and servicing of new consumer routers in the US.</p><p>"We're pleased to share that Netgear is the first retail consumer router company to receive conditional approval from the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) as a trusted consumer router company," Netgear CEO CJ Prober <a target="_blank" class="no-affiliate-link link" href="https://www.netgear.com/letter-from-the-ceo-fcc-conditional-approval/" data-i13n="elm:context_link;elmt:doNotAffiliate;cpos:3;pos:1">said in a statement</a>. "As a US founded and headquartered company, Netgear is aligned with the vision for a more secure digital future for our customers. For the last thirty years, we have been, and continue to be, committed to leading the consumer router category for the United States and setting the bar for quality, performance, innovation and security."</p><p>Both Netgear's lines of Nighthawk and Orbi mesh routers are covered by the approval until October 1, 2027, which appears to mean that the company can continue to offer software updates to both lines and presumably release and sell new models in the future.</p><p>The FCC dramatically expanded the <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.fcc.gov/supplychain/coveredlist#conditional-approvals" data-i13n="cpos:4;pos:1">Covered List</a>, a collection of communications equipment seen as posing a risk to national security, to cover all foreign-made routers in March 2026. The decision prevents companies who make routers outside of the US from introducing new foreign-made models, and pushing certain software updates to existing models after March 1, 2027. Confusingly, though, it doesn't require anyone to replace their existing router or prevent those companies from selling routers they've already made. Receiving conditional approval is the definitive way companies can get off the list, but part of the FCC's requirements for approval is the company offering a plan to bring some or all of its manufacturing to the US — a theoretically costly decision.</p><p>Engadget has contacted Netgear for information about the US manufacturing plan it included in its application for conditional approval. We'll update this article if we hear back.</p><p>The vast majority of router companies, even ones that are headquartered in the US like Netgear, build their routers in Asia. It's not clear what makes Netgear's currently foreign-made routers safer than, say, an <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/home/smart-home/eero-pro-7-review-paying-to-be-sold-a-subscription-171502767.html" data-i13n="cpos:5;pos:1">Amazon Eero 7</a> or a <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/nest-wifi-pro-review-googles-wifi-6e-mesh-is-more-approachable-than-the-rest-130029818.html" data-i13n="cpos:6;pos:1">Google Nest WiFi Pro</a>. Until other companies are given conditional approval, though, Netgear is in a unique position.</p>This article originally appeared on Engadget at https://www.engadget.com/cybersecurity/fcc-just-handed-netgear-a-de-facto-router-monopoly-in-the-us-223712324.html?src=rss

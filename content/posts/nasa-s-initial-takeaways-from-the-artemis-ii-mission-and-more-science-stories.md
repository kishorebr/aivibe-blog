<p>Now that <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/science/space/the-artemis-ii-astronauts-are-back-after-a-10-day-journey-around-the-moon-033800654.html" data-i13n="cpos:1;pos:1">Artemis II is all wrapped up</a>, NASA has begun its post-game performance analyses of all the systems that worked together to get four astronauts safely to the moon and back earlier this month. In addition to taking humans farther than ever before, Artemis II served as a crucial test flight for upcoming crewed missions that are planned for as soon as 2027 and 2028, the latter being <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/science/nasa-overhauls-artemis-program-delaying-moon-landing-to-2028-164255318.html" data-i13n="cpos:2;pos:1">NASA&#39;s ambitious target</a> for landing astronauts on the lunar surface. So far, the Orion spacecraft and the SLS rocket seem to have fared pretty well.&nbsp;</p><p> <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.nasa.gov/missions/nasa-on-track-for-future-missions-with-initial-artemis-ii-assessments/" data-i13n="cpos:3;pos:1">NASA</a> says its initial assessments of the crew capsule show its heat shield &quot;performed as expected, with no unusual conditions identified,&quot; and it didn&#39;t exhibit as much char loss as seen in the uncrewed Artemis I test. (Navy divers snapped some really cool pictures of the heat shield underwater after splashdown, as seen below). Splashdown went according to plan, with Orion landing 2.9 miles from its targeted landing site, according to NASA, and its entry interface velocity &quot;was within one mile-per-hour of predictions.&quot;</p><figure><img src="https://d29szjachogqwa.cloudfront.net/images/user-uploaded/heatshield_1081.jpg" data-crop-orig-src="https://d29szjachogqwa.cloudfront.net/images/user-uploaded/heatshield_1081.jpg" style="height:4000px;width:6000px;" alt="The heat shield from the Orion spacecraft as photographed underwater by divers after its splashdown" data-uuid="0f023f93-1f7b-449f-a816-e0b8a1d0bb72"/><figcaption></figcaption><div class="photo-credit">US Navy</div></figure><p>NASA says the SLS rocket performed well, too. It still has tests to run, but, &quot;At main engine cutoff, when the core stage’s RS-25 liquid engines shutdown, the spacecraft was traveling at over 18,000 miles per hour, achieving its insertion velocity for orbit, and executing a precise bullseye for its intended location,&quot; the space agency noted in a blog post.</p><p>One thing that  we know <em>did</em> cause some issues, though, was the toilet system. Shortly after launch, the astronauts reported <a target="_blank" class="link" href="https://www.engadget.com/science/space/the-latest-on-the-artemis-ii-mission-to-the-moon-and-more-science-stories-160000539.html" data-i13n="cpos:4;pos:1">problems with the urine vent line</a>, which mission specialist Christina Koch was able to troubleshoot with help from the ground crew. But, everyone would like to avoid that on the next mission, so NASA now has teams checking out the hardware and data to identify what went wrong and how to prevent it.&nbsp;</p><h2 id="jump-link-watch-the-earthset">Watch the Earthset</h2><p>The Artemis II astronauts have continued to share glimpses into their journey around the moon, and this week, the mission&#39;s commander, Reid Wiseman posted an incredible video of the Earth setting behind the moon, as seen from the Orion spacecraft.

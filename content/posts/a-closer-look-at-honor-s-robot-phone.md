<p>While Honor has already made plenty of product announcements, with tablets, foldables and more, its most interesting device at MWC 2026 is the <a data-i13n="cpos:1;pos:1" href="https://www.engadget.com/mobile/smartphones/honor-is-making-a-smartphone-with-a-fold-out-camera-201555268.html">Robot Phone</a> — and maybe the humanoid robot that came alongside it.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p>After briefly showing off a model at CES, Honor isn&#39;t quite ready to launch its Robot Phone. However, we got more specs, tech demos and a closer look following the company&#39;s MWC press event in Barcelona. The Robot Phone is currently set to launch later this year.</p>
<span id="end-legacy-contents"></span><figure><img src="https://s.yimg.com/os/creatr-uploaded-images/2026-03/3cfe05b0-157a-11f1-bf7f-02d093a874de" data-crop-orig-src="https://s.yimg.com/os/creatr-uploaded-images/2026-03/3cfe05b0-157a-11f1-bf7f-02d093a874de" style="height:1441px;width:2560px;" alt="Honor Robot Phone at MWC 2026" data-uuid="7676b2a1-9094-34ea-bd3b-bce1d927b954"><figcaption></figcaption><div class="photo-credit">Image by Mat Smith for Engadget</div></figure>
<p>Honor has put a lot of effort into ensuring its camera gimbal is highly mobile, to the point of creating a tiny personal robot that is, dare I say, adorable? The Robot Phone&#39;s pop-up camera can cock its head, shake to say no, nod to agree, and even &quot;flip&quot; – or at least rotate 360 degrees. According to Honor&#39;s presentation, it can even bop along to songs. A spokesperson told me that it&#39;s got five songs in its repertoire, so it&#39;s not clear whether they&#39;re programmed for these kind of demos, or will be a feature of the final retail device.</p>
<p>Another demo here at MWC showed how you could make the Robot Phone &quot;sleep&quot; by covering its gimbal eye, though it&#39;s odd that the camera is still exposed rather than folded away. My main concern with the Robot Phone is the robustness and durability of its robotic mechanisms. We&#39;ve lived through several waves of smartphones that attempted much simpler mechanical camera functions and the threat of dust or heavy-handed users can&#39;t be ignored.</p>
<figure><img src="https://s.yimg.com/os/creatr-uploaded-images/2026-03/3cbd2c20-157a-11f1-927f-37003a1b2f75" data-crop-orig-src="https://s.yimg.com/os/creatr-uploaded-images/2026-03/3cbd2c20-157a-11f1-927f-37003a1b2f75" style="height:1441px;width:2560px;" alt="Honor Robot Phone at MWC 2026" data-uuid="129c903a-1371-3606-b094-7f3df83deb1b"><figcaption></figcaption><div class="photo-credit">Image by Mat Smith for Engadget</div></figure>
<p>The company says it&#39;s taken what it learned from foldables, regarding high-performance materials and simulation accuracy, and applied it to shrinking the camera module. On stage, Honor CEO James Li revealed what he calls the industry&#39;s smallest micro motor, much smaller than a 1-euro coin and, he added,  70 percent smaller than existing micro motors.&nbsp;</p>
<p>As this component has been reduced substantially, the Robot Phone&#39;s gimbal will be the industry&#39;s smallest 4-degrees-of-freedom gimbal system. That&#39;s a spec – we finally got a spec! It&#39;ll also offer three-axis stabilization in this tiny camera package, with the primary camera using a 200-megapixel sensor.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The fold-away panel that the primary camera tucks into also reveals more typical cameras, so you&#39;re not forced to use the gimbal if you don&#39;t need it. Still, that&#39;s one very thick camera unit:</p>
<figure><img src="https://s.yimg.com/os/creatr-uploaded-images/2026-03/3cd65970-157a-11f1-87fc-c02b2a13890c" data-crop-orig-src="https://s.yimg.com/os/creatr-uploaded-images/2026-03/3cd65970-157a-11f1-87fc-c02b2a13890c" style="height:1441px;width:2560px;" alt="Honor Robot Phone at MWC 2026" data-uuid="dff5bb35-310e-3ed1-89b0-f7d492e39f15"><figcaption></figcaption><div class="photo-credit">Image by Mat Smith for Engadget</div></figure>
<p>Honor has already started building out camera modes and features, with a Super Steady Video mode that enhances stability while swinging the Robot Phone around to capture video. AI Object Tracking will apparently intelligently follow subjects, while AI SpinShot supports intelligent 90-degree and 180-degree rotational movement for more cinematic transitions. We&#39;ve seen these sorts of pre-programmed movements and functions in full-size phone gimbals and action cams. If Honor can nail it in such a tiny form, it&#39;ll be impressive.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Other specifications during Honor&#39;s press event were sparse, although the company announced a collaboration with ARRI Image Science to bring its cinematic smarts to the Robot Phone&#39;s gimbal camera.&nbsp;</p>
<p>In a press release, Honor&#39;s Li said the collaboration would bring ARRI&#39;s &quot;cinematic standards and professional workflows&quot; into mobile imaging. It&#39;s apparently the first time elements of ARRI Image Science are being integrated into a consumer device. Dr. Benedikt von Lindeiner, VP at ARRI, said the goal is to bring a true cinematic aesthetic, such as &quot;natural color, gentle highlight roll-off, and a sense of depth,&quot; to shooting with an Honor smartphone.</p>

<p>Honor also made a humanoid robot companion for its Robot Phone. The bot took to the stage alongside the Robot Phone, danced alongside human dancers, did a backflip and shook hands with CEO James Li. It didn&#39;t say a thing, but fortunately, during some on-the-rails banter between the robot, Robot Phone and Honor&#39;s CEO, the Robot Phone was particularly chatty.</p>
<p>Like the many humanoid robots we&#39;ve reported on and seen in person, Honor hopes to put it to work in both industrial and domestic settings, pitching it as a central part of the company&#39;s multi-million-dollar push into AI. For now, it&#39;s being called Honor Robot.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>This article originally appeared on Engadget at https://www.engadget.com/mobile/smartphones/a-closer-look-at-honors-robot-phone-145935198.html?src=rss
